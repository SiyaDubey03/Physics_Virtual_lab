EXP — 01

Measuring Instruments

Learn to use Vernier Calipers, Screw Gauge, Spectrometer, and Travelling Microscope. Practice precision measurements and calculate least counts, zero errors, and corrected readings.

Aim: To study the use of Vernier Calipers, Screw Gauge, Spectrometer and Travelling Microscope and take measurements of various objects.
Vernier Caliper

A Vernier caliper measures length with a precision up to 0.1mm or 0.02mm depending on the vernier scale divisions.

LC = 1 MSD − 1 VSD = S/n

Where S = value of one main scale division, n = number of vernier scale divisions.

Typical Least Count
0.1 mm
Screw Gauge

A screw gauge (micrometer) measures very small dimensions with a precision of 0.01mm using a screw mechanism.

LC = Pitch / Total divisions on thimble

Typical pitch = 1mm, thimble divisions = 100, giving LC = 0.01mm

Typical Least Count
0.01 mm
Spectrometer

A spectrometer is used to measure the angle of refraction, deviation, and for determining the wavelength of light using a prism or grating.

n = sin((A+D)/2) / sin(A/2)

Where A = angle of prism, D = angle of minimum deviation.

Travelling Microscope

A travelling microscope is used to measure small lengths by recording the positions of the microscope cross-wire as it is moved from one point to another.

Length = Final Reading − Initial Reading

Least count is typically 0.001 cm (0.01 mm).

Key Formulae
Vernier Reading
Total = MSR + (VSR × LC)
Screw Gauge Reading
Total = PSR + (HSR × LC)
Corrected Reading
R = Total Reading − Zero Error
Experimental Procedure
1
Clean the jaws of the Vernier caliper and check for zero error by closing the jaws completely and noting the zero of the Vernier scale with respect to the main scale.
2
Place the object (cylinder, sphere etc.) between the jaws and tighten gently until firm contact. Do not over-tighten.
3
Note the Main Scale Reading (MSR) — the last complete division on the main scale just before the zero of the Vernier scale.
4
Find the Vernier Scale Reading (VSR) — the division on the Vernier scale that exactly coincides with any main scale division.
5
Calculate: Total Reading = MSR + (VSR × Least Count). Apply zero error correction.
6
Repeat measurements at different positions of the object. Take 5 readings and calculate mean.
7
For Screw Gauge: Note Pitch Scale Reading (PSR) and Head Scale Reading (HSR). Apply pitch corrections.
8
For Spectrometer: Set up collimator and telescope. Place prism on table. Find angle of minimum deviation for the given spectral line.
9
For Travelling Microscope: Focus on the object. Note the initial reading, move to next point, note final reading. Length = Final − Initial.

EXP — 02

Newton's Rings

Study the phenomenon of interference of light using Newton's Rings apparatus and determine the radius of curvature of a plano-convex lens.

Principle: Newton's Rings are formed by the interference of light waves reflected from the upper and lower surfaces of the air wedge formed between a plano-convex lens and a flat glass plate.
Theory

When a plano-convex lens is placed on a flat glass plate with the curved surface down, an air film of gradually increasing thickness is formed. Monochromatic light incident on this system produces alternate bright and dark concentric rings.

D²ₙ = 4nλR

For dark rings: Dₙ²= 4nλR
Dₙ = diameter of nth dark ring, λ = wavelength, R = radius of curvature

Radius of Curvature Formula
R = (D²ₘ − D²ₙ) / 4(m−n)λ
DₘDiameter of mth dark ring
DₙDiameter of nth dark ring
λWavelength of light used
RRadius of curvature (to find)
Procedure
1
Clean the plano-convex lens and the flat glass plate. Place the lens with its curved surface on the glass plate.
2
Place the arrangement in the sodium lamp light path. Adjust the 45° inclined glass plate to make the light fall normally on the lens.
3
Look through the traveling microscope and locate the interference rings. Focus until the rings are sharp and clear.
4
Move the microscope cross-wire to the extreme left. Record the micrometer reading at each dark ring while moving to the right.
5
Continue recording readings for rings n, n+1, ... m on both sides of the center.
6
Calculate diameters: D = (right reading) − (left reading) for each ring.
7
Use the formula R = (Dₘ² − Dₙ²) / 4(m−n)λ to calculate the radius of curvature.

EXP — 03

Diffraction Grating

Determine the wavelength of light by studying the diffraction pattern produced by a plane diffraction grating using a spectrometer.

Aim: To determine the wavelength of a spectral line using a plane diffraction grating by measuring the angle of diffraction at different orders.
Diffraction Equation
d sin θ = nλ
dGrating element = 1/N (m)
NLines per meter on grating
θAngle of diffraction
nOrder of diffraction
λWavelength of light
λ = d sin θ / n
Apparatus
1
Spectrometer with collimator and telescope
2
Plane diffraction grating (15000 or 6000 lines/inch)
3
Mercury vapour lamp (light source)
4
Spirit level for alignment
Procedure
1
Set up the spectrometer. Level it using a spirit level. Focus the collimator for parallel rays and the telescope for infinity.
2
Mount the grating on the prism table with the ruled surface facing the collimator. Make the grating perpendicular to the collimator.
3
Illuminate the slit with mercury lamp. Turn on the source and observe the central bright image through the telescope.
4
Set the cross-wire on the central image (n=0). Note the vernier reading as the reference position (θ = 0).
5
Rotate the telescope to the first order (n=1) on the left. Set the cross-wire on the spectral line. Record vernier readings V1 and V2.
6
Similarly note readings for first order on the right. Angle θ = (left reading − right reading) / 2
7
Repeat for second order (n=2). Calculate λ = d sinθ / n for each case and find the mean.

EXP — 04

Four Probe Method

Determine the electrical resistivity of a semiconductor (Germanium) sample using the four probe method and study its variation with temperature.

Principle: In the four probe method, current is passed through the outer two probes and voltage is measured across the inner two probes. This eliminates contact resistance errors.
Theory

The resistivity is given by:

ρ = 2πs × (V/I) × f(W/s)

For large wafer (W >> s): f(W/s) ≈ 1

ρ = 2πs × (V/I)
ρResistivity (Ω·cm)
sProbe spacing (cm)
VVoltage across inner probes (V)
ICurrent through outer probes (A)
Temperature Dependence

For a semiconductor, resistivity varies exponentially with temperature:

ρ = ρ₀ · exp(Eg / 2kT)

Taking log:

ln(ρ) = ln(ρ₀) + Eg/(2k) × (1/T)

A graph of ln(ρ) vs 1/T gives a straight line with slope = Eg/2k, from which band gap Eg can be found. Temperature T is in Kelvin.

Procedure
1
Place the Germanium crystal sample on the insulating pad. Ensure the four probes are collinear and equally spaced (s ≈ 2mm typically).
2
Connect the outer two probes (1 and 4) to the constant current source. Connect the inner two probes (2 and 3) to the millivoltmeter.
3
Set the temperature of the oven to room temperature (~303 K). Allow system to stabilize.
4
Set a constant current I (e.g., 5 mA) using the current source. Note the voltage V shown by the millivoltmeter.
5
Calculate ρ = 2πs(V/I). Record ρ and temperature T (in Kelvin).
6
Increase temperature by 10 K steps up to ~453 K. Record V and I at each temperature step.
7
Plot ln(ρ) vs 1/T (K⁻¹). Calculate band gap from slope: Eg = 2k × slope.
